What ABA Therapy Providers Accept Medicaid? (NY, NJ & NC Guide)

September 15, 2024 6 min read By The Match Care ABA Team

If your child is enrolled in Medicaid, you may be wondering whether quality ABA therapy is actually accessible to your family — or whether the best providers only accept private insurance. The answer is more encouraging than many families expect. Medicaid covers ABA therapy in New York, New Jersey, and North Carolina, and there are excellent providers who accept it. This guide explains exactly how Medicaid ABA coverage works in each state and how to find a provider who accepts your plan.

Does Medicaid Cover ABA Therapy?

Yes — in most cases, Medicaid does cover ABA therapy for children with an autism spectrum disorder (ASD) diagnosis. This coverage stems from federal law: under Medicaid's Early and Periodic Screening, Diagnostic and Treatment (EPSDT) benefit, states are required to cover all medically necessary services for children under age 21. Because ABA therapy is widely recognized as medically necessary for children with autism, it falls squarely within this mandate.

In practice, this means that if your child has an autism diagnosis and is enrolled in Medicaid — whether through fee-for-service Medicaid or a Medicaid Managed Care plan — ABA therapy should be a covered benefit. The specifics of how that coverage works, and which providers participate, vary by state. Here is what families in our three service states need to know.

Key fact: Medicaid ABA coverage is not charity care — it is a legal entitlement for eligible children. If your child qualifies for Medicaid and has an autism diagnosis, you have a right to access ABA therapy services through your plan.

ABA Therapy and Medicaid: State-by-State Breakdown

🗽 New York

In New York, ABA therapy is covered under Medicaid for children with an autism diagnosis. Most Medicaid-enrolled children in New York are served through Medicaid Managed Care Organizations (MCOs) such as Healthfirst, Fidelis Care, MetroPlus, Affinity Health Plan, and others. Each MCO has its own network of ABA providers, so the first step is to contact your specific MCO to request a list of in-network ABA providers near you. New York's Office for People With Developmental Disabilities (OPWDD) also administers waiver programs that can fund additional services for children with developmental disabilities, including those with autism. For children under age three, New York's Early Intervention Program provides services regardless of insurance status and does not require a prior autism diagnosis.

🌿 New Jersey

New Jersey's Medicaid program, called NJ FamilyCare, covers ABA therapy for children with autism. Like New York, most New Jersey Medicaid beneficiaries receive their coverage through managed care plans — including Horizon NJ Health, Aetna Better Health of New Jersey, Amerigroup, and WellCare. Each plan contracts with a different set of ABA providers, so coverage and availability vary by the plan your family is enrolled in. New Jersey also has a robust Division of Developmental Disabilities (DDD) that administers Medicaid waiver programs for individuals with developmental disabilities. These waivers can provide funding for ABA therapy and related supports beyond what standard Medicaid covers, and they are particularly valuable for families whose children need intensive services.

🌲 North Carolina

North Carolina transitioned to a Medicaid Managed Care model in 2021, which means most Medicaid-enrolled children are now covered through one of four managed care plans: Aetna Better Health of North Carolina, Carolina Complete Health, Healthy Blue, or WellCare of North Carolina. All four plans are required to cover ABA therapy for children with autism. North Carolina also has the Innovations Waiver, which provides home and community-based services for individuals with intellectual or other developmental disabilities. The Innovations Waiver can cover behavioral supports and ABA-related services for eligible individuals, though there is often a waitlist for waiver slots. Families who cannot wait may also explore the Basic Plus Waiver, which has a shorter waitlist.

How to Find ABA Providers That Accept Medicaid

Finding an ABA provider that accepts your specific Medicaid plan — and is currently taking new clients — is often the hardest part of the process. Here is a practical approach that works:

Step 1: Identify Your Managed Care Plan

If your child is enrolled in Medicaid, find out which managed care plan (MCO) administers their benefits. This information is on your child's Medicaid card or in the welcome packet you received when they were enrolled. If you are unsure, you can call your state Medicaid office to confirm.

Step 2: Call the MCO's Member Services Line

Contact your MCO's member services line and ask for a list of in-network ABA therapy providers in your zip code. Be specific: ask for providers who offer Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) therapy for children with autism. Ask about any prior authorization requirements and what documentation you will need to submit (typically an autism diagnosis letter, physician referral, and functional behavior assessment).

Step 3: Verify Availability Directly

Provider directories maintained by MCOs are frequently out of date. Once you have a list of supposedly in-network providers, call each one directly to confirm they are still accepting patients on your specific plan and have current openings. This is time-consuming but essential — many families waste weeks going through an intake process only to discover the provider's Medicaid contract is no longer current.

Step 4: Ask About Waitlists

Even Medicaid-accepting providers often have waitlists. Ask each provider you contact how long the current waitlist is for new Medicaid clients. If the wait is long, ask whether you can get on the waitlist now while continuing to search for alternatives.

Step 5: Check for Dual Coverage

If your child has both Medicaid and private insurance (through an employer, for example), they have what is called "dual coverage." In this situation, the private insurance pays first, and Medicaid acts as the secondary payer — often covering whatever remaining cost share the private insurance leaves behind. This can effectively eliminate out-of-pocket costs for ABA therapy. Make sure any ABA provider you work with knows about both coverage sources.

What to Do If a Provider Says They Do Not Accept Medicaid

Some ABA providers do not participate in Medicaid networks, either because Medicaid reimbursement rates are lower than private insurance rates or because the administrative burden of billing Medicaid is significant. This is a real barrier, but it is not insurmountable. Here are your options:

  • Keep searching. Not all providers have the same contracting decisions. There are quality ABA practices that do accept Medicaid and have invested in the infrastructure to work within those programs. They exist in all three of our service states.
  • Ask about scholarship or sliding-scale options. Some providers who do not accept Medicaid still offer reduced-fee services for families with financial need. It is always worth asking.
  • Explore Medicaid waiver programs. If standard Medicaid is not opening doors, your state's waiver programs (OPWDD in NY, DDD in NJ, Innovations Waiver in NC) may fund ABA services through a different mechanism that more providers participate in.
  • File a complaint if needed. If your child's Medicaid plan is unable to provide a timely list of available in-network ABA providers, that may constitute a network adequacy violation. Contact your state's Medicaid office or insurance commissioner to report the issue.

What Documentation You Need for Medicaid ABA Authorization

Whether you have private insurance or Medicaid, the documentation requirements for ABA prior authorization are largely similar. Gather the following before starting the intake process:

  • Your child's formal autism spectrum disorder diagnosis letter, signed by a licensed clinician, including the DSM-5 diagnostic code (F84.0)
  • A physician referral for ABA therapy stating it is medically necessary for autism spectrum disorder
  • Your child's Medicaid ID number and managed care plan information
  • Any previous assessments, IEPs, or therapy records that document your child's behavioral needs

Once you have chosen an ABA provider, their intake team will typically complete the functional behavior assessment (FBA) and submit the prior authorization request to your Medicaid plan. The authorization timeline for Medicaid can vary — budget two to six weeks for a decision, though some plans process requests faster.

Important: Medicaid-funded ABA therapy carries the same quality standards as privately insured ABA. All ABA programs must be supervised by a Board Certified Behavior Analyst (BCBA) regardless of how the services are funded. Do not let the funding source lead you to expect less — advocate for the same quality of care every child deserves.
