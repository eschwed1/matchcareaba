Is ABA Therapy Covered by Insurance? What Parents Need to Know

The short answer is yes — for most families in the United States, ABA therapy is covered by insurance. Thanks to a combination of state autism insurance mandates and federal Medicaid law, the vast majority of children with autism spectrum disorder are entitled to ABA therapy coverage. But the details matter: which plan you have, which state you live in, which providers are in-network, and how to navigate prior authorization. This guide explains all of it.

The Short Answer: Yes, For Most Families

As of 2024, all 50 states have some form of autism insurance mandate requiring coverage for autism-related services, including ABA therapy. Federal law (through the Affordable Care Act and Medicaid's EPSDT provision) adds another layer of protection, particularly for children. If you have commercial insurance or Medicaid and your child has an autism diagnosis, ABA therapy is almost certainly a covered benefit. The question is usually not whether you're covered — but how to find an in-network provider and navigate the authorization process.

Federal Law and State Mandates

Federal + State Framework

Layered Legal Protections for ABA Coverage

ABA coverage is protected at multiple levels. The Affordable Care Act (ACA) requires all ACA-compliant plans to cover Essential Health Benefits, and autism services — including ABA — are included in many states' essential health benefit benchmarks. The Autism CARES Act has reinforced federal attention to autism services and research.

State autism insurance mandates are the most direct protection for most families with commercial insurance. These state laws vary in their strength — some have no dollar caps, no visit limits, and clear ABA mandates; others are more limited. New York (2011), New Jersey (2009), and North Carolina (2015) all have among the stronger mandates in the country, with clear requirements for ABA coverage with no dollar caps.

What NY, NJ, and NC Insurance Plans Cover

New York

NY's Autism Insurance Mandate

New York law requires all commercial insurance plans to cover ABA therapy for children with autism with no dollar limits and no age cap. NY Medicaid, Child Health Plus, and all Medicaid Managed Care plans (HealthFirst, MetroPlus, Fidelis, Affinity, Molina) are required to cover ABA as well. NY's mandate is among the nation's strongest.

New Jersey

NJ's Autism Insurance Mandate

New Jersey passed its autism insurance mandate in 2009 — one of the first states to do so. All NJ commercial plans must cover ABA with no dollar limits. NJ FamilyCare (Medicaid) covers ABA through its managed care plans: Horizon NJ Health, Amerigroup, Aetna Better Health, and WellCare. NJ's CHIP program also provides coverage.

North Carolina

NC's Autism Insurance Mandate

North Carolina passed its autism insurance mandate in 2015, requiring all state-regulated commercial insurance plans to cover ABA. Blue Cross Blue Shield NC, Cigna, Aetna, and UnitedHealthcare all must cover ABA. NC Medicaid covers ABA through its Tailored Plans for eligible individuals with autism and behavioral health needs.

Medicaid and ABA Therapy

Medicaid Coverage

Federal Law Requires Medicaid to Cover ABA

Under Medicaid's Early and Periodic Screening, Diagnostic, and Treatment (EPSDT) provision, all state Medicaid programs are required to cover medically necessary treatments — including ABA therapy — for children under 21. This federal requirement applies in all 50 states and cannot be waived by individual states. All Medicaid Managed Care plans (the plans most Medicaid beneficiaries are enrolled in) are bound by the same requirement.

How to Verify Your ABA Insurance Coverage

  • Call the member services number on your insurance card and ask specifically about "applied behavior analysis" or "ABA therapy" benefits
  • Ask about Prior Authorization requirements — most plans require PA before ABA services begin
  • Request a list of in-network ABA providers in your area (but verify their current availability independently)
  • Ask about out-of-network coverage if no in-network providers are available near you
  • Get the names of the representatives you speak with and document what you're told
  • Ask specifically about any session limits, hour caps, or age restrictions on ABA coverage
